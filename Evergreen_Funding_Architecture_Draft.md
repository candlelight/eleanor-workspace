## Executive Summary

This document synthesizes extensive research on impact investing, social enterprise financing, and underestimated founder outcomes into a concrete evergreen funding architecture. The core insight: the "missing middle" ($50K–$1M) isn't a capital scarcity problem—it's an access architecture problem. This design addresses that gap through a revolving, founder-governed capital pool targeting underestimated founders who deliver 30% higher returns but receive <2% of venture capital.

## Part II: Capital Architecture

### The Evergreen Mechanism: A Revolving Blended Structure

This fund operates as a **revolving capital pool**—not a closed-end fund with fixed liquidation. This enables true patient capital with continuous recycling and reinvestment.

### Capital Stack by Tranche

| Tranche | Source | % of Fund | Return Profile | Role |
|---------|--------|-----------|----------------|------|
| **Catalytic Capital** | Foundations, DAFs, HNWIs | 15% | First-loss, 0–2% coupon | Absorb downside, enable leverage, signal credibility |
| **Institutional Capital** | Pension funds, family offices, impact investors | 55% | 5–7% target, time-locked 10yr | Core deployment capital, financial sustainability |
| **Recycling Pool** | Repayments, revenue-share returns, successful exits | 25% | Reinvested perpetually | Evergreen engine, patient capital source |
| **Platform Reserve** | Fund operations, platform R&D, ecosystem building | 5% | N/A | Infrastructure sustainability, not overhead |

### Recycling Mechanics

**1. Primary Recycling**
Portfolio repayments (debt, revenue-share, recoverable grant returns) flow to Recycling Pool, not returned to LPs. This pool is perpetually reinvested.

**2. Secondary Recycling**
20% of exit proceeds above return threshold flow to Recycling Pool rather than full LP distribution. This creates compounding impact over time.

**3. Governance Lock**
Recycling Pool allocations decided by Impact Committee (founder-majority structure, see Governance section below).

### Investment Instrument Mix

Deployment via portfolio approach matched to founder stage and business model:

| Instrument | Allocation | Target Return | Target Stage | Founder Profile |
|------------|------------|---------------|--------------|-----------------|
| **Recoverable Grants** | 40% | 0–3% | Pre-revenue, high-risk | Underestimated founders at inflection point 2 (first check) |
| **Revenue-Share Agreements** | 35% | 8–15% | Revenue-generating, growth | Inflection point 5 (next round bridge) |
| **Subordinated Debt** | 20% | 6–10% | Established SEs, operational | Operational evolution phase |
| **Program-Related Investments** | 5% | 0% current, equity upside | Strategic platform infrastructure | Ecosystem enablers, secondary market infrastructure |

### Liquidity Profile

**For Founders:**
- No fixed exit timeline required
- Repayment via revenue, profit share, or secondary sale to fund
- Option to convert revenue-share to equity if venture-scale path emerges
- Access to follow-on capital from Recycling Pool based on performance, not arbitrary stage gates

**For Limited Partners:**
- 10-year lock-up with annual redemption windows (5% of commitment) after year 5
- Target distributions begin year 4 from operational cash flows
- Secondary market: LP interests transferable to qualified impact investors with fund approval

**For Recycling Pool:**
- Perpetual; no return obligations
- Compounding growth through reinvestment
- Creates "evergreen" capital base independent of fundraising cycles

## Part III: Governance Structure

### Democratic Capital with Founder Agency

Traditional fund governance mirrors corporate power concentration: GP controls, LP commits, founder receives. This structure inverts that dynamic.

### Three-Chamber Governance Model

#### Chamber 1: Impact Committee
- **Composition:** 5 members
  - 3 portfolio founders (rotating, 2-year terms, elected by founder cohort)
  - 1 independent impact measurement expert
  - 1 fund GP (non-voting unless tiebreaker needed)
- **Authority:** 
  - Allocations from Recycling Pool ($ decisions)
  - Strategic direction and theory of change
  - Impact standards and measurement framework
  - Ecosystem partner selection and accreditation
  - Fund policy changes affecting portfolio

#### Chamber 2: Investment Committee
- **Composition:** 5 members
  - 3 fund GPs
  - 2 independent capital markets experts (impact investing background)
- **Authority:**
  - Day-to-day deployment decisions
  - Due diligence and underwriting
  - Terms negotiation within mandate
  - Portfolio monitoring and founder support
  - Quarterly reporting to Capital Council

#### Chamber 3: Capital Council
- **Composition:** LP representatives + advisory voices
- **Authority:**
  - Quarterly review rights
  - Audit and compliance oversight
  - Advisory input on strategy (non-binding)
  - Right to propose policy changes (Impact Committee votes)

### Founder Majority Principle

Key decisions affecting portfolio companies require Impact Committee majority. This includes:
- Recycling Pool allocations (where capital flows next)
- Follow-on investment decisions for existing portfolio
- Changes to impact measurement framework
- Ecosystem partner selection
- Fund policy evolution

### Transparency Mechanisms

- **Portfolio Observer Rights:** All portfolio companies have quarterly observer rights at Investment Committee meetings (non-voting)
- **Open-Book Annual Session:** Full fund financials disclosed to all portfolio founders annually
- **Conflict Disclosure:** Any GP with potential competing interest must abstain and disclose; recorded in meeting minutes
- **Founder Cohort Calls:** Monthly peer sessions facilitated by fund (governance-free zone for founder support)

### Term Structure

| Role | Term | Renewal |
|------|------|---------|
| Investment Committee (GPs) | 7 years | Renewable with Capital Council approval |
| Investment Committee (Independents) | 3 years | Maximum 2 terms |
| Impact Committee (Founders) | 2 years | Rotating, cannot serve consecutive terms |
| Impact Committee (Expert) | 3 years | Renewable |

## Part IV: Impact Measurement Framework

### The Thesis: Founder Resilience + Economic Outcomes

Traditional impact measurement (IRIS+, IMM) focuses on sector-specific outcomes (CO2 reduced, jobs created). This framework adds founder-centric metrics validating the underestimated thesis.

### Three-Layer Measurement

#### Layer 1: Founder Resilience Metrics
Track the McKinsey-identified barriers:
- **Network Access:** Warm intros facilitated, peer connections made
- **Bias Mitigation:** Incidents of advocacy on founder's behalf
- **Blueprint Support:** Hours of operational/skill-building support delivered
- **Mental Health:** Founder-reported stress/burnout (validated instrument), access to coaching

#### Layer 2: Business Performance Metrics
Standard venture metrics without the exit pressure:
- Revenue growth (validated against comparable ventures)
- Unit economics progression
- Team diversity metrics
- Customer retention/cohort analysis
- Gross margins and path to self-sustainability

#### Layer 3: Sector-Specific Impact
Aligned to venture's stated mission:
- Environmental: CO2, waste diverted, materials recycled
- Social: Jobs in underserved communities, wage levels vs. living wage, supplier diversity
- Governance: Employee ownership %, board diversity, transparency practices

### Reporting Cadence

| Frequency | Audience | Content |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| Quarterly | Investment Committee | Financial performance, red flags, support needs |
| Quarterly | Impact Committee | Resilience metrics, founder cohort health |
| Semi-annual | Capital Council | Full portfolio performance, impact synthesis |
| Annual | Public | Anonymized portfolio data, lessons learned |

### The "Bend, Don't Break" Principle

Measurement should inform, not burden. Founders report quarterly on standardized dashboard (15 min). Fund staff conducts annual deep-dive (half-day workshop). No metric that doesn't inform capital allocation decision should be collected.

## Part V: 30-Day Prototype Plan

### Objective: Validate the Mechanism, Not the Model

This sprint tests the core hypothesis: *Will underestimated founders engage with a patient, revolving capital structure run by peers?*

### Week 1–2: Founder Discovery Sprint

**Goal:** Validate inflection point pain with 8–10 underestimated founders (women, BIPOC, or intersectional)

**Method:**
- 45-minute structured interviews
- Focus areas:
  - Which of the 6 inflection points almost killed their venture?
  - What would "patient capital" mean in practice? (no fixed exit timeline, operational support, peer network)
  - Would they serve on an Impact Committee that governs fund allocations?
  - What terms would make revenue-share or recoverable grants attractive vs. traditional equity/debt?

**Success Metrics:**
- 6+ founders express willingness to join governance structure
- 3+ cite "next round bridge" (inflection point 5) or "first check gap" (inflection point 2) as critical pain point
- 2+ express skepticism about traditional VC terms

**Deliverable:** Founder Insight Brief (3 pages): anonymized quotes, patterns, validated pain points

### Week 3: Capital Structure Validation

**Goal:** Test blended finance appetite with 3–5 potential capital sources

**Method:**

**Foundations/DAFs:**
- Pitch first-loss catalytic capital concept (15% tranche)
- Gauge interest in recoverable grant mechanics
- Test "evergreen recycling" narrative vs. traditional grant-making

**Family Offices:**
- Pitch 10-year lock-up, 5–7% return, impact-aligned structure
- Test revulsion (or attraction) to "no fixed exit" for portfolio companies
- Explore interest in founder-governance model

**Institutional Investors:**
- Early exploratory conversations (not pitching)
- Gauge regulatory constraints on evergreen structures
- Assess appetite for underestimated founder thesis

**Success Metrics:**
- 1+ foundation expresses written interest in first-loss participation
- 1+ family office requests follow-on materials or second meeting
- Identification of regulatory barriers for institutional capital

**Deliverable:** Capital Appetite Memo (2 pages): sources contacted, interest levels, barriers identified

### Week 4: Ecosystem Coalition Mapping

**Goal:** Map ecosystem and identify partners who could co-design

**Method:**

**Underestimated-Founder Funds/Accelerators:**
- Reach out to 10 organizations (Kapor Capital, Harlem Capital, Backstage Capital, Digital Undivided, etc.)
- Explore co-investment or referral partnerships
- Test interest in governance participation (Impact Committee roles)

**Entrepreneur Support Organizations (ESOs):**
- Connect with 2–3 ESOs who could administer recoverable grants (Delta model)
- Assess their capacity and interest in revolving fund mechanics
- Explore shared services for portfolio support

**Secondary Market Infrastructure:**
- Conversations with 2–3 impact fund secondaries specialists
- Assess feasibility of LP interest transfers
- Gauge appetite for evergreen fund structures in secondary market

**Success Metrics:**
- 2+ organizations agree to exploratory partnership call
- 1+ expresses potential co-investment or referral interest
- Identification of 1 ESO with capacity for recoverable grant administration

**Deliverable:** Ecosystem Map (visual + narrative): 10 organizations mapped by partnership potential

### 30-Day Deliverables Summary

| Deliverable | Format | Purpose |
|-------------|--------|---------|
| Founder Insight Brief | 3-page memo | Validate pain points and governance interest |
| Capital Appetite Memo | 2-page memo | Validate capital stack feasibility |
| Ecosystem Map | Visual + 2-page narrative | Identify coalition partners |
| Go/No-Go Decision | 1-page recommendation | Proceed, pivot, or pause |

### Decision Framework

**Proceed to 90-Day Design Phase if:**
- Founder demand validated (6+ interested in governance participation)
- Capital interest confirmed (1+ foundation or family office commits to continued conversation)
- Ecosystem partners identified (2+ potential referral/co-investment partners)

**Pivot Mechanism if:**
- Different pain points emerge (e.g., founders want traditional equity with longer timelines, not revenue-share)
- Governance model meets resistance (founders skeptical of peer governance)
- Capital sources want traditional closed-end structure

**Pause if:**
- Structural barriers prove insurmountable (regulatory, legal, or tax constraints on evergreen structures)
- Founder cohort shows no interest in alternative capital structures
- No capital sources express continued interest
